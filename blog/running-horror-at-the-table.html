Running Horror at the Table: A Practical Guide

Matthias Moore
July 2, 2026
6 min read

Horror is the hardest genre to run at a table, and it isn't close. A novel controls every word you read; a film controls every frame you see. You control neither. Your players talk over the tense silence, crack a joke at the worst possible moment, and roll a natural 20 that trivializes the monster you spent a week building. The medium itself fights you.

But that same lack of control is exactly why tabletop horror, when it lands, lands harder than any other medium. The dread is happening to them, in real time, with their own choices pulling the noose tighter. Here's how I try to get there.

Dread Is a Pace, Not an Event

New DMs tend to treat horror as a moment — the jump scare, the reveal, the thing bursting through the door. Those are the payoff, not the genre. The genre is the twenty minutes before the door, when nothing has happened yet and the players can feel that something is about to.

So slow down. Spend real table time on the ordinary: the innkeeper who won't meet anyone's eye, the barn with fresh straw but no animals, the child's shoe in the road with no child. Let the party notice these things themselves. Fear you point at isn't scary; fear they assemble is.

Design Principle

Horror lives in the gap between what the players know and what they suspect. Your job is to widen that gap and hold it open as long as you can.

Telegraph, Then Deliver

Unfair horror isn't frightening — it's just frustrating. If the ghoul in the crypt can drop a character from full health with no warning, players don't feel dread; they feel cheated, and they stop engaging. The fix is to telegraph. Show the aftermath before the cause: the clawed grooves in the stone, the older graves already dug open, the villager who went in and came back wrong.

By the time the threat actually arrives, the table has spent an hour imagining it — and their imagination has built something worse than any stat block you could write. Crucially, they walked in anyway. That's the trick. Fair warning is what converts a cheap surprise into a dreadful choice.

"The scariest monster is the one the players describe to each other in the ten minutes before they open the door."

Protect the Unknown

Every mechanic you expose is a candle lit in a dark room. The moment players know it's a vampire, they know the whole checklist — sunlight, running water, an invitation — and mystery collapses into logistics. So withhold the name. Describe effects, not stat entries: they don't fight "a wight," they fight the thing that was the miller last week and is still wearing his face.

This goes for dice, too. Ask for a Wisdom save without saying why. Narrate the success as "you feel, for no reason you can name, that you should not have touched that." Uncertainty is the resource you're spending all night — don't give it away for free.

Safety Is What Makes Horror Possible

This sounds backwards, so let me be plain: the tables that go to the darkest places are the ones that talked first. In session zero, ask what's off the table — harm to children, body horror, whatever it is for your group — and mean it. Keep an X-card or a "lines and veils" agreement within reach, and honor it instantly and without comment when someone uses it.

Players don't lean into fear when they're quietly bracing against a real one. Knowing the exits exist is precisely what lets them stop looking for them and sink into the story. Safety tools aren't the opposite of horror — they're the permission slip for it.
