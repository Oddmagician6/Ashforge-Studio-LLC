Building Edenfall: A World Designed for Drop-In Play

Matthias Moore
April 15, 2026
5 min read

The hardest thing about running a published setting isn't the rules — it's the homework. Before you can run Faerun or Eberron with confidence, you feel like you need to read the sourcebooks, understand the political factions, know the gods. Most DMs never get past that feeling. They either run something they know by heart, or they homebrew everything from scratch.

Edenfall is my answer to that problem. It's a setting designed around the assumption that the DM has read exactly one thing: the adventure in front of them.

The Drop-In Principle

Edenfall is built on a simple premise: a DM should be able to read a single adventure, run it cold, and have everything they need in front of them. The setting details aren't a prerequisite — they're a reward. Every adventure plants seeds. The bigger world is something the table discovers, not studies.

Design Principle

Setting details are rewards for play, not prerequisites for it. The table earns the lore by running the adventure.

This means each adventure in the Edenfall Collection is written to be self-contained. Every NPC has what they need in the module. Every location has enough history to feel real without requiring outside knowledge. The wider world is implied, not mandatory.

What Edenfall Actually Is

At its core, Edenfall is a region in decline. Old institutions are crumbling. New powers are filling the gaps. The roads are less safe than they used to be, and the wilderness has grown strange. It's a world where the party matters — not because they're chosen heroes, but because everyone else is too busy surviving to act.

The feel is dark pastoral: small communities, deep woods, old ruins with unclear histories. There are no metropolises in the core collection. The scale is deliberately human. The threats are local. The stakes feel personal.

Design Philosophy

Every location, faction, and NPC in Edenfall is written with two questions in mind: what do they want, and what are they afraid of? That's it. Everything else — the politics, the history, the magic — flows from those two questions. It keeps the world grounded and makes it easy to improvise when players go off-script.

"What do they want, and what are they afraid of? Those two questions are the whole setting."

I borrowed this approach from literary fiction writers, not game designers. Characters in good novels feel real because their motivations are legible. I wanted Edenfall NPCs to have that same quality — not complex for complexity's sake, but coherent in a way that makes them easy to play.

Where It Goes From Here

The Crooked Mile is the first adventure in the collection, but it's not the first story. There are things happening in the wider region that predate the players' arrival. Some of those threads run through the entire collection. Others are left open for tables to take wherever they want.

Volume 2 is in active development. It takes place in a different part of the region entirely — a location that has nothing to do with the road through the woods — but players who ran The Crooked Mile will recognize something in it. That's intentional.

If you want to follow the development of Edenfall as it grows, Patreon is where the behind-the-scenes work happens. I post design notes, early drafts, and table reports for every adventure before it goes to final layout.
