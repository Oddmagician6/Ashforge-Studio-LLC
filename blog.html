The Crooked Mile Is Here: What It Is and Why I Made It

The Edenfall Collection started as a single encounter and grew into something I couldn't stop writing. Here's the origin of The Crooked Mile and what comes next.

Every supplement I've ever run at my home table started as a single image in my head. For The Crooked Mile, it was a road through the woods that didn't end where it should. The party walks in. They walk for hours. The trees don't change. The light doesn't shift. And eventually they start to realize the road is taking them somewhere they didn't choose.

Why Edenfall?

I wanted to build a setting that rewarded drop-in play. Most campaign worlds ask Dungeon Masters to do a lot of reading before they can run confidently. Edenfall is designed the other way: you run the adventure, and the setting reveals itself through play. The Crooked Mile is the first entry point.

What's Next

The Edenfall Collection will grow one adventure at a time. Each volume is self-contained but leaves threads for a longer arc if the table wants to follow them. Volume 2 is already in development. Patreon supporters will see it first.

Building Edenfall: A World Designed for Drop-In Play

Most settings ask DMs to learn a world before running it. Edenfall is designed the other way around - you run it first, then the world reveals itself.

The hardest thing about running a published setting isn't the rules - it's the homework. Before you can run Faerun or Eberron with confidence, you feel like you need to read the sourcebooks, understand the political factions, know the gods. Most DMs never get past that feeling.

The Drop-In Principle

Edenfall is built on a simple premise: a DM should be able to read a single adventure, run it cold, and have everything they need in front of them. The setting details aren't a prerequisite - they're a reward. Every adventure plants seeds. The bigger world is something the table discovers, not studies.

What Edenfall Actually Is

At its core, Edenfall is a region in decline. Old institutions are crumbling. New powers are filling the gaps. The roads are less safe than they used to be, and the wilderness has grown strange. It's a world where the party matters - not because they're chosen heroes, but because everyone else is too busy surviving to act.

Design Philosophy

Every location, faction, and NPC in Edenfall is written with two questions in mind: what do they want, and what are they afraid of? That's it. Everything else - the politics, the history, the magic - flows from those two questions. It keeps the world grounded and makes it easy to improvise when players go off-script.
